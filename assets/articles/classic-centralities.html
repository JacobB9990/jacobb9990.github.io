Brief Dive About: Classic Centrality Measures

Why Centrality?

In a network, which nodes matter the most? Well that depends. Some networks might have a different definition of what is important. For instance, on a platform like X (formerly Twitter) A user with millions of followers has a high degree centrality. They have the ability to broadcast information instantly to many people. In this case, "importance" is the ability to reach many others directly. But let us consider another user. They have fewer followers, but are positioned between two otherwise disconnected communities. That user may have high betweenness centrality. Here, importance could mean controlling the flow of information between groups. So even within the same network. Degree captures broadcast power and betweenness captures brokerage power. So, we can see there are different definitions of importance.

In 1978, Linton C. Freeman formalized three major notions of importance: Degree, Closeness, and Betweenness. Later, spectral methods like eigenvector centrality and PageRank expanded the idea of "importance." Centrality is a mapping that assigns importance to each node. $$C:V \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$$

Degree Centrality - Local Importance

As I hinted before, Degree Centrality is the number of direct connections a given node has It is purely local. We count the neighbors and is a proxy for direct influence. The formal definition is: $$C_{D}(i)= k_{i}$$ where \( k_{i} \) denotes the number of neighbors node \( i \). The strengths of this measure is the simplicity and cheapness to compute. The weakness is that it ignores global structure.

Closeness Centrality - Distance-Based Importance

Closeness Centrality measures how close a node is to all other nodes in the network. It is global. We compute the shortest path distance from node \( i \) to every other node and take the reciprocal of the total distance. The formal definition is: $$C_{C}(i)= \frac{1}{\sum_{j} d(i,j)}$$ where \( d(i,j) \) is the shortest path distance between nodes \( i \) and \( j \). The strengths of this measure is that it captures how fast information can spread from a node under shortest path assumptions. The weakness is that it assumes information travels along geodesics and it does not behave well in disconnected graphs.

Betweenness Centrality - Control of Flow

Betweenness Centrality measures how often a node lies on shortest paths between other pairs of nodes. It is global. We count the fraction of shortest paths between all pairs that pass through node \( i \). The formal definition is: $$C_{B}(i)= \sum_{s \ne i \ne t} \frac{\sigma_{st}(i)}{\sigma_{st}}$$ where \( \sigma_{st} \) is the number of shortest paths from node \( s \) to node \( t \) and \( \sigma_{st}(i) \) is the number of those paths that pass through node \( i \). The strengths of this measure is that it detects bridges and brokerage positions in the network. The weakness is that it assumes shortest path routing and is computationally expensive on large graphs.

Eigenvector Centrality - Prestige

Eigenvector Centrality assigns importance to a node based on the importance of its neighbors. It is global and recursive. A node is important if it connects to other important nodes. The formal definition is given by the eigenvector equation: $$Ax = \lambda x$$ where \( A \) is the adjacency matrix of the network and \( x \) is the centrality vector. The strengths of this measure is that it captures prestige and influence beyond immediate neighbors. The weakness is that it can concentrate importance in dense regions and may fail in certain directed or disconnected graphs.

RageRank - Random Walk Centrality

PageRank Centrality is a modification of eigenvector centrality based on a random walk with teleportation. It is global. The centrality of a node is proportional to the centrality of nodes linking to it, scaled by their out degree and a damping factor. The formal definition is: $$PR(i)= \alpha \sum_{j \to i} \frac{PR(j)}{k^{out}_{j}} + \frac{1-\alpha}{N}$$ where \( \alpha \) is the damping factor, \( k^{out}_{j} \) is the out degree of node \( j \), and \( N \) is the total number of nodes. The strengths of this measure is that it is stable on directed networks and has a probabilistic interpretation as the stationary distribution of a random walk. The weakness is that it still does not explicitly model diffusion dynamics and depends on the choice of damping parameter.

Conclusion

These centrality measures give different answers to the same fundamental question of what it means for a node to be important. Some are purely local while others incorporate global structure. Some rely on shortest paths while others rely on spectral or random walk interpretations. There is no single correct notion of importance. The appropriate definition depends on the structure of the network and the process taking place on it. In many modern applications such as influence maximization and contagion modeling, classical centralities serve as useful heuristics, but they do not explicitly model the underlying dynamics. Understanding their assumptions and limitations is therefore just as important as computing them.
